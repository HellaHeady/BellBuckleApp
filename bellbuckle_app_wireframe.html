📐 Wireframe · Our History Page
The Story of Bell Buckle
From railroad crossroads to National Register landmark · Est. 1852
1
Interactive Timeline
1852 · The Founding
Nashville & Chattanooga Railroad Arrives
A. D. Fuggitt donates land for rail line. Bell Buckle becomes a vital stop between Nashville and Chattanooga.
1870s–1900s · The Boom
Major Stockyard — Population Tops 1,000
Bell Buckle becomes the largest stockyard between Nashville and Chattanooga. Downtown thrives. Webb School founded 1870.
1915–1950s · The Quiet Years
Automobiles & Decline
Rise of the car erodes rail commerce. Most downtown storefronts board up. The town grows quiet but not forgotten.
1960s · The Resurgence
Restoration Wave Begins
Appreciation for Bell Buckle's quaint Victorian heritage sparks a wave of purchases, rentals, and building renovations. Artisans and creatives move in.
1976 · Recognition
National Register of Historic Places
During the Bicentennial, the downtown and Webb School area earns National Register listing. Bell Buckle is official.
1978 · Daffodil Day
Daffodils & Community Tradition Born
Sawney Webb (Webb School founder) first planted daffodils along 6 miles of highway as a discipline for students. In the 1940s, janitor Eugene Brady Sr. expanded the plantings. By 1978 it became an annual festival — Daffodil Day — held every 3rd Saturday in March.
Today · Community
Arts, Culture & Small-Town Pride
Home to RC Cola & Moon Pie Festival (30 years!), Webb School Art & Craft Show (800+ booths), Quilted Christmas, Farmers Market, Bell Buckle Arts Council, and a thriving creative community. Tagline: "Close to home, but a world away."
2
Chapter Spotlights
Chapter 1
The Railroad Boom
When the N&C line came through in 1852, it didn't just bring trains — it brought prosperity, commerce, and community. Bell Buckle became the heartbeat of the Nashville-to-Chattanooga corridor.
Read More →
Chapter 2
The 1960s Resurgence
After decades of quiet, Bell Buckle woke up. Artists, craftspeople, and history lovers discovered its Victorian bones and chose to stay. The restoration movement that began in the 60s saved the soul of this town.
Read More →
Chapter 3
Historic Preservation
The Victorian storefronts, churches, and homes you see today didn't survive by accident. Dedicated residents and organizations fought to preserve Bell Buckle's architectural heritage for future generations.

What is Bee City USA?

Bee City USA is a program of the Xerces Society for Invertebrate Conservation — a national nonprofit that certifies cities, towns, and campuses committed to protecting native pollinators through habitat, education, and reduced pesticide use.

Certified communities pledge to: create and protect pollinator habitat, minimize pesticide use, raise public awareness about native bees, and support bee-friendly ordinances.

Did You Know?
There are over 400 native bee species in Tennessee — most don't sting, don't make honey, and nest in the ground or in hollow stems.
Our Town's Commitment
Bell Buckle's Bee Story

Bell Buckle earned its Bee City USA certification through the hard work of local gardeners, the Bell Buckle Arts Council, and community volunteers who transformed roadside areas and town gardens into pollinator corridors.

The certification means our town officially commits each year to expanding habitat, hosting pollinator education events, and protecting the native bees that keep our gardens, farms, and wildflowers healthy.
